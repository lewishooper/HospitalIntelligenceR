# Government Policy and Accreditation Standards That Shape Ontario Hospital Strategic Plans
## A Research Reference Document
*HospitalIntelligenceR — April 2026*

---

## Purpose

This document summarizes the principal Ontario government policies and Accreditation
Canada standards that create strategic planning pressures on Ontario public hospitals.
Understanding these external forces is essential context for interpreting patterns in
hospital strategic plans — particularly the convergence of themes across institutions
of different sizes and types.

---

## Part 1 — Ontario Government Policy

### 1.1 System Integration and Ontario Health Teams

**The Connecting Care Act, 2019** (S.O. 2019, c. 5, Sched. 1) is the most
consequential legislation shaping current hospital strategy. It:

- Dissolved the 14 Local Health Integration Networks (LHINs) and transferred their
  functions to Ontario Health, a new provincial "super agency"
- Established Ontario Health Teams (OHTs) as the primary vehicle for integrated care
  delivery — voluntary collaborations of health service providers organized around
  geographic populations
- Gave Ontario Health authority to fund, monitor, and hold accountable all health
  service providers, including hospitals, through service accountability agreements
- Gave the Minister authority to require integrations between health service providers
  and to issue directives to hospitals

As of January 2024, Ontario Health approved the 58th Ontario Health Team, achieving
full provincial coverage so that every part of Ontario is served by an OHT.

**Strategic planning implication:** Hospitals are now expected to demonstrate
alignment with their OHT, including shared planning, integrated service delivery, and
seamless patient transitions. This directly explains the high prevalence of
Partnerships & Integration (PAR) directions in strategic plans across all hospital
types — it is not merely aspirational language but a contractual expectation embedded
in the HSAA.

---

### 1.2 Quality Improvement and the Excellent Care for All Act

**The Excellent Care for All Act, 2010** (ECFAA) (S.O. 2010, c. 14) remains a
cornerstone of Ontario hospital quality accountability. It requires all public
hospitals to:

- Develop and publicly post an annual Quality Improvement Plan (QIP), submitted
  to Ontario Health by April 1 each year
- Establish a quality committee of the board, responsible for monitoring and
  reporting on quality issues including the annual QIP
- Conduct annual surveys of patients and staff on satisfaction with care and services
- Link CEO and senior executive compensation to the achievement of QIP
  performance targets
- Develop a patient declaration of values following public consultation
- Establish a patient relations process

The QIP program was first implemented in Ontario hospitals in 2011 and has since
been extended to primary care, home and community care, and long-term care. The
board chair must sign the QIP, certifying board approval and acknowledging the
board's oversight responsibility. QIPs are submitted via Ontario Health's QIP
Navigator platform.

**Strategic planning implication:** QIP indicator areas — patient safety, access to
care, integration, patient experience — are systematically reflected in strategic
direction language. Because QIP targets must be linked to executive pay, they have
operational weight that shapes what hospitals include in their strategic plans.

Research on the ECFAA's impact found that while most hospital leaders viewed it
positively, some CEOs of smaller hospitals reported that mandatory indicators
reflected provincial priorities that were not always relevant to their local context,
and that QIP compliance requirements sometimes distracted boards from their
organization's own quality strategy (Barnsley et al., 2012).

### 1.3 Financial Accountability

Several legislative instruments govern hospital financial accountability:

- **Balanced budget requirement** — the HSAA requires all hospitals to achieve an
  annual balanced budget position, defined as total revenues greater than or equal
  to total expenses, consistent with Public Sector Accounting Board (PSAB) rules
- **Broader Public Sector Accountability Act, 2010** (BPSAA) — prohibits hospitals
  from hiring lobbyists using public funds; requires public reporting on executive
  expenses; mandates annual board attestation of compliance; brought hospitals under
  the Freedom of Information and Privacy Act (FIPPA)
- **Public Sector Salary Disclosure Act, 1996** — requires annual disclosure of all
  employees earning $100,000 or more (the "Sunshine List")
- **Broader Public Sector Compensation Act, 2014** — imposes constraints on executive
  compensation frameworks; Regulation 304/16 governs executive compensation
  frameworks for hospitals

**Strategic planning implication:** The mandatory balanced budget requirement, combined
with chronic underfunding pressures and post-COVID recovery costs, makes financial
sustainability a near-universal strategic priority across all hospital types. This is
not a strategic choice — it is a legal obligation.

### 1.4 Equity, Diversity, Inclusion, and Anti-Racism

Ontario Health released its Equity, Inclusion, Diversity and Anti-Racism (EIDAR)
Framework in 2020, establishing system-level expectations for health equity across
all funded organizations including hospitals. Key elements include:

- Expectations that hospitals collect and use race-based and other demographic data
  to identify and address health inequities
- Integration of equity indicators into QIP planning and reporting
- Expectations for workforce diversity and inclusive practices
- Alignment with Ontario's broader Anti-Racism Strategy

Beginning with the 2020-21 planning period, equity was formally embedded in Ontario
Health's expectations for health service providers through the EIDAR framework.
Ontario hospitals have since released individual equity, diversity, and inclusion
strategies and anti-racism plans, many triggered or accelerated by the COVID-19
pandemic's documented disproportionate impact on racialized communities.

**Strategic planning implication:** The EIDAR framework and QIP equity indicators
explain the emergence of Equity, Diversity & Inclusion (EDI) directions in plans
from the 2021–2025 generation. The prevalence of EDI directions is both a values
signal and a policy compliance response.

### 1.5 Digital Health and Technology

Ontario has made digital health a strategic priority across the health system:

- **Your Health: A Plan for Connected and Convenient Care** (2023) — the Ministry
  of Health's current strategic plan, emphasizing digital access, virtual care, and
  data-driven healthcare; hospitals are expected to align planning with this framework
- **Digital Health Playbook** (2024) — sets out provincial digital health standards
  and expectations for Ontario Health Teams and their member organizations, including
  hospitals; requires OHTs to use provincially funded digital assets from the Digital
  Health Service Catalogue before procuring market solutions
- Over $106 million in funding was allocated to digital and virtual care projects
  for OHTs and health care providers through the federal-provincial Shared Health
  Priorities agreement (2023–2026)
- As of 2023, Ontario Health's Cyber Security Model has engaged 100 per cent of
  acute hospitals through ten regional cyber security shared service partnerships;
  Bill 194 (Strengthening Cyber Security and Building Trust in the Public Sector
  Act, 2024) received Royal Assent November 25, 2024, establishing a framework for
  cyber security, data protection, and responsible AI use

**Strategic planning implication:** Innovation and Digital Health (INN) directions in
hospital strategic plans reflect both genuine organizational interest and the
provincial expectation that hospitals participate in Ontario's digital health
infrastructure. The Innovating Digital Health Solutions Program (2023–2024) funded
13 pilot projects aligned with the "Digital First for Health" strategy.

---

### 1.6 Health Human Resources

Workforce shortages represent one of the most acute pressures on Ontario hospitals,
and provincial policy has directly shaped strategic responses:

- **Your Health: A Plan for Connected and Convenient Care** names hiring more health
  care workers as one of its three core pillars
- Ontario has invested in nursing education supports including the Bridging
  Educational Grant in Nursing program; as of May 2023, over 1,000 participants
  had enrolled
- The Ministry committed an additional $965 million in hospital operating funding
  for 2024–25, including targeted support for small and northern hospitals facing
  health human resources shortages and emergency department closures
- The Canada-Ontario Agreement on Shared Health Priorities (2023–26) includes
  investments in health workforce development

**Strategic planning implication:** The near-universal presence of Workforce & Culture
(WRK) directions in hospital strategic plans is inseparable from the province-wide
workforce crisis. Recruitment, retention, and staff wellbeing have become existential
strategic priorities, not aspirational ones.

### 1.7 Governance Requirements

Hospital governance in Ontario is governed by multiple overlapping legislative
requirements:

- **Public Hospitals Act, R.S.O. 1990** — requires hospitals to be governed by a
  board of directors; requires establishment of a medical advisory committee and
  fiscal advisory committee; gives the Minister authority to issue directives to
  boards; permits the Lieutenant Governor in Council to appoint a supervisor
- **Ontario Not-for-Profit Corporations Act, 2010** (ONCA) — proclaimed in force
  October 19, 2021; became the foundational corporate governance legislation for
  Ontario hospitals; introduced enhanced conflicts management requirements;
  hospitals that had not transitioned by October 18, 2024 are now deemed to comply
- **HSAA governance provisions** — the HSAA requires board chairs to certify
  compliance with performance obligations; under the Public Hospitals Act, section 4,
  all changes to hospital constating documents now require Ministry of Health approval

## Part 2 — Accreditation Canada

### 2.1 Overview of the Qmentum Program

Accreditation Canada has provided health system accreditation services in Canada
since 1958 and is accredited by the International Society for Quality in Health Care.
The organization is a not-for-profit independent body affiliated with the Health
Standards Organization (HSO), which develops the standards that Accreditation Canada
applies. The program is trusted by over 9,300 health and social service locations
across Canada.

The Qmentum accreditation program is the primary framework used by Ontario hospitals.
In 2024, Accreditation Canada transitioned its Canadian clients to **Qmentum Global**,
an updated program that:

- Moves away from the traditional "big bang" accreditation preparation model to a
  more continuous, adaptive learning approach
- Introduces short-notice on-site survey visits alongside the traditional scheduled
  survey cycle
- Incorporates attestations and continuous quality improvement action planning
  throughout the cycle rather than concentrating preparation before surveys
- Requires participation in the HSO Global Workforce Survey™ as a mandatory component
  starting in 2024
- Includes the HSO Governing Body Assessment as a standard survey instrument

Beginning in April 2024, Accreditation Canada also took on a new mandate from the
Ontario Government as the independent quality assessment body for over 900 community
surgical and diagnostic centres in Ontario.

---

### 2.2 The Qmentum Quality Framework

Accreditation Canada's quality framework defines quality across eight dimensions:

- Population focus
- Accessibility
- Safety
- Work life (workforce)
- Client-centred services
- Continuity of services
- Effectiveness
- Efficiency

These eight dimensions directly map to themes observable in hospital strategic plans.
The alignment is not coincidental — hospitals preparing for accreditation use these
dimensions to frame their quality improvement work, which in turn influences strategic
planning language.

---

### 2.3 Required Organizational Practices (ROPs)

Qmentum includes Required Organizational Practices (ROPs) — high-priority practices
with evidence of risk reduction for patients, staff, and organizations. ROPs are
mandatory; failure to meet them affects the accreditation decision. ROPs span areas
including:

- Patient safety (medication reconciliation, hand hygiene, surgical safety checklists,
  fall prevention, pressure injury prevention)
- Client and family-centred care
- Workforce safety and wellness
- Information management and data quality
- Governance and leadership accountability

**Strategic planning implication:** ROPs create baseline quality expectations that
hospitals must meet regardless of their strategic priorities. Directions related to
patient safety, quality, and workforce reflect in part the operational reality of
maintaining ROP compliance.

---

### 2.4 Accreditation and Strategic Planning

The Qmentum program explicitly includes strategic planning as an assessed domain.
Surveyors assess:

- Whether the organization has a current strategic plan
- Whether board governance structures and processes support the strategic direction
- Whether quality improvement activities are aligned with strategic priorities
- Whether there is demonstrable leadership accountability for strategic outcomes

The OHA's Guide to Good Governance (4th edition) explicitly states that hospital
boards should monitor strategic plan objectives as part of their performance
management framework, and that mandatory indicators from the HSAA and QIP should be
incorporated into the board's performance monitoring framework.

An accreditation report for North York General Hospital (Qmentum Global, October
2024) noted the hospital's "deeply ingrained culture of safety and quality" and
"strong collaborations with community partners" as accreditation strengths — language
that directly reflects strategic planning priorities under the PAT and PAR taxonomy
codes used in this research.

---

### 2.5 Indigenous Health Accreditation

Accreditation Canada has developed a dedicated accreditation program for
Indigenous-serving organizations that recognizes traditional healing and wellness
as essential elements of quality care. This program represents an emerging standard
for hospitals with significant Indigenous patient populations and is relevant context
for EDI directions focused on Indigenous cultural safety.

## Part 3 — Analytical Implications

The policy and accreditation landscape described above explains several key features
of the strategic direction distribution observed in this research:

- **PAR (Partnerships & Integration) is the third-largest theme at 16%** because
  Ontario Health Team membership and system integration are contractual HSAA
  obligations, not discretionary priorities
- **FIN (Financial Sustainability) appears across all hospital types** because the
  balanced budget requirement is a legal obligation; no hospital can afford not
  to have a financial sustainability direction
- **WRK (Workforce & Culture) is the largest theme at 20%** because the province-wide
  health human resources crisis has made workforce retention and wellbeing existential
  issues, reinforced by provincial investment commitments and QIP workforce indicators
- **EDI (Equity, Diversity & Inclusion) appears in 27 directions** representing a
  meaningful but not universal adoption — consistent with a framework that encourages
  but does not yet fully mandate equity directions in HSAAs and QIPs for all hospitals
- **INN (Innovation & Digital Health) appears in 35 directions** — consistent with
  the provincial digital health agenda, though uptake varies by hospital capacity
  and OHT digital maturity

The convergence of strategic themes across Ontario hospitals is therefore not
surprising — it is a structural feature of a tightly regulated, publicly funded
system in which contractual obligations, accreditation standards, and provincial
policy priorities all push in the same direction.
