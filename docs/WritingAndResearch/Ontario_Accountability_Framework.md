# The Ontario Hospital Accountability Agreement Framework
## A Summary for Research Context
*HospitalIntelligenceR — April 2026*

---

## What It Is

The accountability agreement framework is the primary contractual mechanism through
which the Ontario government funds and oversees public hospitals. It operates as a
cascading structure: the Ministry of Health holds Ontario Health accountable through
a Ministry-Ontario Health Accountability Agreement, and Ontario Health in turn holds
individual hospitals accountable through Hospital Service Accountability Agreements
(HSAAs).

---

## Legislative Foundation

- **Connecting Care Act, 2019** (S.O. 2019, c. 5, Sched. 1) — the primary enabling
  legislation; replaced the Local Health System Integration Act, 2006; established
  Ontario Health as the provincial agency responsible for funding, planning, and
  accountability across the health system; proclaimed in force June 6, 2019
- **Public Hospitals Act** (R.S.O. 1990, c. P.40) — foundational statute governing
  hospital governance, requiring boards of directors, medical advisory committees,
  and fiscal advisory committees; gives the Minister authority to issue operational
  directives to hospital boards
- **Excellent Care for All Act, 2010** (S.O. 2010, c. 14) — requires all public
  hospitals to develop annual Quality Improvement Plans (QIPs), establish quality
  committees, link executive compensation to QIP performance targets, and conduct
  patient and staff satisfaction surveys

---

## The Hospital Service Accountability Agreement (HSAA)

- Every Ontario public hospital is required to negotiate an HSAA with Ontario Health
- The HSAA is typically a two-year contract that stipulates accountability and
  performance obligations for planning, integration, and delivery of programs and
  services
- Key HSAA obligations include:
  - Achieving performance standards set out in the Agreement's schedules
  - Submitting multi-year planning submissions to Ontario Health
  - Achieving a balanced budget position and meeting financial and
    performance obligations
  - Notifying Ontario Health of any significant changes to clinical services
  - Participating in integration initiatives as directed
- The agreements set out the rights and obligations with respect to
  funding and the process for planning for funding each fiscal year
- If a hospital fails to meet performance standards, Ontario Health may require an
  Improvement Plan and, if improvement does not occur, may appoint an independent
  review team

---

## The Quality Improvement Plan (QIP)

- Required annually under the Excellent Care for All Act, 2010
- Submitted to Ontario Health via the QIP Navigator platform by April 1
  of each year
- Hospitals must:
  - Engage patients in QIP development
  - Publicly post the QIP
  - Link executive compensation to QIP performance targets
  - Have the QIP signed by the board chair, certifying board approval
- QIPs cover indicators in areas including patient safety, access, quality, and —
  since 2012–13 — integration and readmission rates

---

## The Ministry-Ontario Health Accountability Agreement

- Establishes the respective obligations of the Ministry and Ontario
  Health relating to key strategic, operational, performance, and funding expectations
- Along with the Connecting Care Act, 2019 and its regulations, these
  documents make up the key elements of the accountability framework between Ontario
  Health and the Ministry of Health
- Ontario Health in turn holds hospitals accountable through the HSAA structure

---

## Broader Public Sector Requirements

- **Broader Public Sector Accountability Act, 2010** (BPSAA) — applies to all
  hospitals receiving provincial funding; prohibits hiring lobbyists using
  public funds; requires public reporting of consultant expenses and certain expenses
  of board members and senior managers; requires attestation by hospital Boards of
  Directors on compliance; brings hospitals under the Freedom of Information and
  Privacy Act (FIPPA)
- **Public Sector Salary Disclosure Act, 1996** — requires annual public disclosure
  of names, positions, and salaries of all employees earning $100,000 or more
- **Ontario Not-for-Profit Corporations Act, 2010** (ONCA) — became the foundational
  corporate governance legislation for Ontario hospitals, proclaimed in force October
  19, 2021; introduced enhanced conflicts management requirements for directors

---

## Relevance to Strategic Plan Analysis

The accountability framework creates direct convergence pressure on hospital strategic
plans through three mechanisms:

- **Performance indicators in HSAAs** must be reflected in hospital planning —
  hospitals are contractually required to achieve them
- **QIP priorities** are set in part by Ontario Health guidance and must be publicly
  posted and board-approved — they influence strategic direction language
- **Balanced budget requirement** is a non-negotiable HSAA obligation — explaining
  the near-universal presence of financial sustainability directions across all
  hospital types

This framework is the primary reason why Ontario hospital strategic plans exhibit
convergence across institutions, as identified in the Denis, Langley & Lozeau
research tradition.
