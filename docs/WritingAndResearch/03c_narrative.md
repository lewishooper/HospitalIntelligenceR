# Strategic Theme Prevalence by Era and Hospital Type
## Ontario Public Hospitals — Era × Type Interaction Analysis
*HospitalIntelligenceR | Analysis 03c | April 2026*

---

## Overview

Analysis 03b established that the thematic composition of Ontario hospital strategic
plans has shifted across three plan eras (2018–2026), with Workforce & People (WRK)
showing the most robust change and Research & Academic (RES) exhibiting an unusual
V-shaped trajectory that warranted further examination. This analysis disaggregates
those findings by hospital type group to determine whether the aggregate patterns hold
uniformly across the sector or are concentrated in specific segments.

Two questions drive this analysis:

1. **WRK:** Does the aggregate rise (69% Pre-COVID → 94% Current) hold across all
   four hospital type groups, or is it driven by one segment?
2. **RES:** Does the V-shape (23% Pre-COVID → 11% Early Recovery → 39% Current)
   reflect a genuine sector-wide pattern, or is it a composition artefact produced
   by changes in which hospital types are represented in each era?

---

## Data and Method

**Cohort:** 113 Ontario public hospitals (two additional hospitals excluded relative
to 03b due to missing era assignment). The analytical cohort, filtering criteria,
era definitions, and prevalence metric are identical to 03b.

**Hospital type groups:**

| Type group | n hospitals | Notes |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| Community — Small | 57 | General acute, rural and small urban |
| Community — Large | 44 | General acute, mid-to-large urban |
| Teaching | 15 | Includes paediatric-specialty hospitals (e.g., SickKids) |
| Specialty | 13 | Chronic & Rehab and Specialty Mental Health; not general acute |

**Era composition by type:** The distribution of hospital types is not uniform across
eras. Pre-COVID is a small cohort (n=13) with proportionally more Teaching and Specialty
representation than the larger Current cohort (n=62). This compositional imbalance is
directly relevant to interpreting the RES finding.

| Era | Teaching | Community — Large | Community — Small | Specialty | Total |
|-----|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|-------|
| Pre-COVID | 2 (15%) | 5 (38%) | 5 (38%) | 1 (8%) | 13 |
| Early Recovery | 3 (8%) | 17 (45%) | 16 (42%) | 2 (5%) | 38 |
| Current | 8 (13%) | 21 (34%) | 23 (37%) | 10 (16%) | 62 |

**Interpretive caution — small cells:** Several era × type cells contain fewer than
five hospitals, particularly in Pre-COVID (Teaching n=2, Specialty n=1). Point estimates
for these cells are reported for completeness but carry wide implicit uncertainty.
Conclusions drawn from these cells are noted accordingly.

## Finding 1: WRK Rise is Broadly Real, with One Notable Exception

WRK prevalence by era and hospital type:

| Type | Pre-COVID | Early Recovery | Current | Shift |
|------|-----------|----------------|---------|-------|
| Community — Large | 80% | 100% | 100% | +20 pp |
| Community — Small | 80% | 81% | 91% | +11 pp |
| Teaching | 50% | 100% | 75% | +25 pp |
| Specialty | 0% | 100% | 90% | +90 pp |

**Community hospitals — the core finding holds.** Both Community — Large and
Community — Small show the expected upward trend. Community — Large hospitals reached
saturation (100%) in the Early Recovery era and maintained it through Current. This
group, representing the largest share of the cohort, anchors the aggregate finding.
Community — Small shows a more gradual but consistent rise, reaching 91% in Current
plans. Across the community acute segment, WRK is effectively a sector standard in
current strategic planning.

**Specialty hospitals — large swing, small Pre-COVID base.** The apparent zero-to-90%
movement for Specialty hospitals should be read with caution: the Pre-COVID cell
contains a single hospital. The Current figure (90%, n=10) is meaningful and consistent
with the sector-wide pattern, but the Pre-COVID zero is not a reliable baseline.

**Teaching hospitals — the exception.** Teaching hospitals show an unexpected pattern:
WRK prevalence rises from 50% (Pre-COVID, n=2) to 100% (Early Recovery, n=3) but
then retreats to 75% in Current plans (n=8). The Current figure is the most reliable
of the three — eight hospitals is sufficient to take seriously. This dip is not simply
noise.

A plausible interpretation is that Teaching hospitals frame workforce priorities
differently. Academic health science centres and paediatric hospitals may embed
workforce strategy within research, education, and academic mission directions rather
than naming it as a standalone WRK direction. This is a structural feature of how
Teaching hospitals organize their strategic plans rather than evidence of a genuine
deprioritization of workforce. The finding warrants closer examination at the
direction-description level but does not undermine the sector-wide WRK conclusion.

**Conclusion on WRK:** The aggregate rise is real and broadly distributed across the
Community hospital segment, which comprises approximately 80% of the cohort. The
Teaching hospital dip in Current plans is a small-n result worthy of note but does
not materially alter the sector-wide interpretation.

## Finding 2: The RES V-Shape is a Composition Artefact

This is the more consequential finding from this analysis, and it supersedes the
provisional RES interpretation offered in 03b.

RES prevalence by era and hospital type:

| Type | Pre-COVID | Early Recovery | Current | Pattern |
|------|-----------|----------------|---------|---------|
| Teaching | 100% | 100% | 88% | High and stable |
| Specialty | 100% | 50% | 70% | High, variable |
| Community — Large | 0% | 0% | 48% | Flat then jump |
| Community — Small | 0% | 0% | 0% | Absent throughout |

The aggregate V-shape (Pre-COVID 23% → Early Recovery 11% → Current 39%) dissolves
completely when the cohort is disaggregated by type. There is no V-shape within any
individual type group. What the aggregate pattern actually reflects is three distinct
structural phenomena occurring simultaneously:

**1. Teaching and Specialty hospitals have always had high RES prevalence.** Both
groups show near-ceiling RES rates in every era. This is expected: academic mandates,
research affiliations, and specialized clinical programs make research and academic
directions structurally appropriate for these hospitals. The slight decline in Teaching
hospitals' Current RES rate (100% → 88%) is consistent with broader plan complexity
and is not analytically significant given the cell sizes involved.

**2. Community — Large hospitals show a genuine Current-era shift.** The jump from
0% to 48% in Community — Large hospitals is a real finding. These hospitals do not
have academic mandates, and the appearance of RES directions in nearly half of their
current plans represents a meaningful change. This likely reflects the growing
importance of research partnerships, quality improvement initiatives framed as
evidence-based practice, and the influence of Ontario Health's emphasis on data-driven
planning. The 48% figure should be watched in future cycles.

**3. Community — Small hospitals show no RES presence in any era.** RES is
effectively absent from this segment across all three eras. This is structurally
coherent: small rural and community hospitals operate under different resource
constraints and strategic mandates. Research is not a realistic priority for a
hospital operating at thin margins with a primary mandate of local acute care
delivery.

**Why the aggregate looked like a V-shape:** The Pre-COVID cohort happened to include
a higher proportion of Teaching and Specialty hospitals (23% combined) relative to
the Early Recovery cohort (13% combined). Because these types always have high RES
rates, their over-representation in Pre-COVID inflated the aggregate baseline. As the
cohort composition shifted toward Community hospitals in Early Recovery (87% combined),
aggregate RES prevalence fell — not because any hospital deprioritized research, but
because a different mix of hospitals was writing plans. The subsequent Current-era
recovery reflects two things: Specialty hospitals returning to higher representation
(16%) and Community — Large hospitals newly adopting RES directions.

**Revised conclusion on RES:** RES is not a sector-wide temporal trend. It is a
structurally stratified theme: near-universal in Teaching and Specialty hospitals,
newly emerging in Community — Large, and absent in Community — Small. The 03b
aggregate finding of a V-shaped trend should not be reported. The more accurate
framing is that RES has expanded its reach within the Community — Large segment
and remains a defining feature of Teaching and Specialty planning.

## Full Theme Heatmap by Type — Additional Observations

Beyond WRK and RES, the full era × type breakdown reveals several additional
patterns worth noting for future analysis:

**PAT and PAR are truly universal.** Patient Care & Quality and Partnerships &
Integration show high prevalence across all type groups in all eras, with no
systematic type-based differentiation. These themes are sector-wide in the
strongest sense.

**FIN shows moderate type differentiation.** Financial Sustainability is more
prevalent in Community hospitals than in Teaching or Specialty, consistent with
the greater financial pressure faced by community acute hospitals operating under
tighter funding envelopes.

**EDI is a Community — Large and Teaching phenomenon.** Equity, Diversity &
Inclusion directions appear primarily in larger, urban hospitals. The Early
Recovery spike seen in 03b is concentrated in Community — Large and Teaching
hospitals; the subsequent retreat in Current plans is also most pronounced in
these groups.

**INN is broadly distributed but stronger in larger hospitals.** Innovation &
Digital Health prevalence is higher in Teaching and Community — Large hospitals,
consistent with the greater infrastructure and resource base required to pursue
digital health transformation meaningfully.

## Methodological Note on This Analysis

The composition table produced by this analysis (03c_era_type_composition.csv)
should be treated as a standard reference document for any future aggregate
temporal findings in this dataset. Before reporting any aggregate Pre-COVID →
Current shift as a sector-wide trend, the type composition of each era should
be checked. The Pre-COVID cohort (n=13, with 23% Teaching/Specialty) is not
compositionally representative of the full Ontario public hospital sector, and
aggregate findings from that baseline are sensitive to which hospitals happened
to be writing plans in 2018–2021.

## Next Steps

1. **Direction-description review — Teaching WRK:** Examine whether Teaching hospitals
   embed workforce content within other direction types (RES, academic mission) in a
   way that suppresses the WRK classification. This would confirm the structural
   explanation for the Current-era dip.

2. **Community — Large RES tracking:** The emergence of RES in 48% of Current-era
   Community — Large plans is a genuine finding worth monitoring in future cycles.
   If this proportion holds or grows, it signals a meaningful shift in how community
   hospitals are framing their relationship with evidence and research.

3. **CIHI quality correlation (future):** Type-stratified theme prevalence provides
   a natural entry point for correlating strategic emphasis with CIHI quality
   indicators within type groups — a more appropriate comparison than cross-type
   analysis.
