# Strategic Theme Prevalence by Era and Hospital Type
## Ontario Public Hospitals — Era × Type Interaction Analysis
*HospitalIntelligenceR | Analysis 03c | May 2026*

---

## Overview

Analysis 03b established that the thematic composition of Ontario hospital strategic
plans has shifted across three plan eras (2018–2026), with Workforce & People (WRK)
showing the most robust change and Research & Academic (RES) exhibiting the largest
aggregate shift (+23.3 pp). This analysis disaggregates those findings by hospital
type group to determine whether the aggregate patterns hold uniformly across the sector
or are concentrated in specific segments.

Two questions drive this analysis:

1. **WRK:** Does the aggregate rise (75% Pre-COVID → 91% Current) hold across all
   four hospital type groups, or is it driven by one segment?
2. **RES:** Does the large aggregate shift reflect a genuine sector-wide pattern, or
   is it a composition artefact produced by changes in which hospital types are
   represented in each era?

---

## Data and Method

**Cohort:** 118 Ontario public hospitals (one additional hospital excluded relative
to 03b due to missing era assignment — FAC 973, year 2015). The analytical cohort,
filtering criteria, era definitions, and prevalence metric are identical to 03b.

**Hospital type groups:**

| Type group | n hospitals | Notes |
|------------|-------------|-------|
| Community — Small | 48 | General acute, rural and small urban |
| Community — Large | 43 | General acute, mid-to-large urban |
| Teaching | 17 | Includes paediatric-specialty hospitals (CHEO, SickKids) |
| Specialty | 11 | Chronic & Rehab and Specialty Mental Health; not general acute |

**Era composition by type:** The distribution of hospital types is not uniform across
eras. Pre-COVID is a small cohort (n=12) with proportionally fewer Teaching hospitals
than the larger Current cohort (n=65). This compositional imbalance is directly
relevant to interpreting the RES finding.

| Era | Teaching | Community — Large | Community — Small | Specialty | Total |
|-----|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|-------|
| Pre-COVID | 1 (8.3%) | 5 (41.7%) | 5 (41.7%) | 1 (8.3%) | 12 |
| Early Recovery | 4 (9.8%) | 18 (43.9%) | 17 (41.5%) | 2 (4.9%) | 41 |
| Current | 12 (18.5%) | 20 (30.8%) | 25 (38.5%) | 8 (12.3%) | 65 |

**Interpretive caution — small cells:** Several era × type cells contain fewer than
five hospitals, particularly in Pre-COVID (Teaching n=1, Specialty n=1). Point estimates
for these cells are reported for completeness but carry wide implicit uncertainty.
Conclusions drawn from these cells are noted accordingly.

## Finding 1: WRK Rise is Broadly Real, with One Notable Exception

WRK prevalence by era and hospital type:

| Type | Pre-COVID | Early Recovery | Current | Shift |
|------|-----------|----------------|---------|-------|
| Community — Large | 80% | 100% | 100% | +20 pp |
| Community — Small | 80% | 82% | 92% | +12 pp |
| Teaching | 100% | 100% | 75% | −25 pp |
| Specialty | 0% | 100% | 88% | +88 pp |

**Community hospitals — the core finding holds.** Both Community — Large and
Community — Small show the expected upward trend. Community — Large hospitals reached
saturation (100%) in the Early Recovery era and maintained it through Current. This
group, representing the largest share of the cohort, anchors the aggregate finding.
Community — Small shows a more gradual but consistent rise, reaching 92% in Current
plans. Across the community acute segment, WRK is effectively a sector standard in
current strategic planning.

**Specialty hospitals — large swing, small Pre-COVID base.** The apparent zero-to-88%
movement for Specialty hospitals should be read with caution: the Pre-COVID cell
contains a single hospital. The Current figure (88%, n=8) is meaningful and consistent
with the sector-wide pattern, but the Pre-COVID zero is not a reliable baseline.

**Teaching hospitals — the exception.** Teaching hospitals show an unexpected pattern:
WRK prevalence is 100% in both Pre-COVID (n=1, interpret with extreme caution) and
Early Recovery (n=4) but retreats to 75% in Current plans (n=12). The Current figure
is the most reliable of the three — twelve hospitals is a sufficient base to take
seriously. This apparent dip is not simply noise.

A plausible interpretation is that Teaching hospitals frame workforce priorities
differently. Academic health science centres and paediatric hospitals may embed
workforce strategy within research, education, and academic mission directions rather
than naming it as a standalone WRK direction. This is a structural feature of how
Teaching hospitals organize their strategic plans rather than evidence of a genuine
deprioritization of workforce. Direction-level review would be needed to confirm
whether WRK content is present but differently labelled.

**Conclusion on WRK:** The aggregate rise is real and broadly distributed across the
Community hospital segment, which comprises approximately 80% of the cohort. The
Teaching hospital reading in Current plans (75%, n=12) is a finding worthy of note
but does not materially alter the sector-wide interpretation.

## Finding 2: The RES Aggregate Shift is a Composition Artefact

This is the more consequential finding from this analysis, and it supersedes the
aggregate RES interpretation offered in 03b.

RES prevalence by era and hospital type:

| Type | Pre-COVID | Early Recovery | Current | Pattern |
|------|-----------|----------------|---------|---------|
| Teaching | 100% | 100% | 92% | High and stable |
| Specialty | 100% | 50% | 63% | High, variable |
| Community — Large | 0% | 6% | 50% | Flat then jump |
| Community — Small | 0% | 0% | 0% | Absent throughout |

The aggregate shift (Pre-COVID 16.7% → Current 40.0%) dissolves completely when the
cohort is disaggregated by type. There is no consistent upward trend within any
individual type group. What the aggregate pattern actually reflects is three distinct
structural phenomena occurring simultaneously:

**1. Teaching and Specialty hospitals have always had high RES prevalence.** Both
groups show near-ceiling RES rates in every era. This is expected: academic mandates,
research affiliations, and specialized clinical programs make research and academic
directions structurally appropriate for these hospitals. The Teaching reading at 92%
in Current (n=12) and Specialty at 63% (n=8) are the most reliable estimates for
their respective groups.

**2. Community — Large hospitals show a genuine Current-era shift.** The jump from
approximately 0% to 50% in Community — Large hospitals is a real finding. These
hospitals do not have academic mandates, and the appearance of RES directions in
half of their current plans represents a meaningful change — likely reflecting the
growing importance of research partnerships, quality improvement initiatives framed
as evidence-based practice, and Ontario Health's emphasis on data-driven planning.
The 50% figure should be watched in future cycles.

**3. Community — Small hospitals show no RES presence in any era.** RES is
effectively absent from this segment across all three eras. This is structurally
coherent: small rural and community hospitals operate under different resource
constraints and strategic mandates. Research is not a realistic priority for a
hospital operating at thin margins with a primary mandate of local acute care delivery.

**Why the aggregate appeared as a large shift:** The Pre-COVID cohort (n=12) contains
one Teaching and one Specialty hospital — types that always have high RES rates —
alongside ten Community hospitals, where RES is near-zero. As the cohort composition
shifted toward an increasing share of Teaching hospitals in the Current era (n=12,
18.5% of era total) and Community — Large hospitals newly adopting RES, the aggregate
rate rose — not because any hospital changed its research posture, but because the
mix of hospitals writing plans in each era differs systematically.

**Revised conclusion on RES:** RES is not a sector-wide temporal trend. It is a
structurally stratified theme: near-universal in Teaching and Specialty hospitals,
newly emerging in Community — Large, and absent in Community — Small. The 03b
aggregate shift of +23.3 pp should not be reported as a sector trend. The more
accurate framing is that RES has expanded its reach within Community — Large and
remains a defining feature of Teaching and Specialty planning.

## Full Theme Heatmap by Type — Additional Observations

Beyond WRK and RES, the full era × type breakdown reveals several additional
patterns worth noting for future analysis:

**PAT and PAR are broadly distributed.** Patient Care & Quality and Partnerships &
Community show high prevalence across most type groups in most eras, with no
systematic type-based differentiation strong enough to disrupt the aggregate readings
from 03b.

**FIN shows moderate type differentiation.** Financial Sustainability is more
prevalent in Community hospitals than in Teaching or Specialty, consistent with
the greater financial pressure faced by community acute hospitals operating under
tighter funding envelopes.

**EDI is concentrated in Community — Large and Teaching.** The Early Recovery spike
seen in 03b is concentrated in Community — Large and Teaching hospitals; the
subsequent retreat in Current plans is also most pronounced in these groups. Community
— Small hospitals have low EDI prevalence throughout.

**INN is stronger in larger hospitals.** Innovation & Digital Health prevalence is
higher in Teaching and Community — Large hospitals, consistent with the greater
infrastructure and resource base required to pursue digital health transformation
meaningfully.

## Methodological Note on This Analysis

The composition table produced by this analysis (03c_era_type_composition.csv)
should be treated as a standard reference document for any future aggregate
temporal findings in this dataset. Before reporting any aggregate Pre-COVID →
Current shift as a sector-wide trend, the type composition of each era should
be checked. The Pre-COVID cohort (n=12, with just one Teaching and one Specialty
hospital) is not compositionally representative of the full Ontario public hospital
sector, and aggregate findings from that baseline are sensitive to which hospitals
happened to be writing plans in 2018–2021.

## Next Steps

1. **Direction-description review — Teaching WRK:** Examine whether Teaching hospitals
   embed workforce content within other direction types (RES, academic mission) in a
   way that suppresses the WRK classification. This would confirm the structural
   explanation for the Current-era reading.

2. **Community — Large RES tracking:** The emergence of RES in 50% of Current-era
   Community — Large plans is a genuine finding worth monitoring in future cycles.
   If this proportion holds or grows, it signals a meaningful shift in how community
   hospitals are framing their relationship with evidence and research.

3. **CIHI quality correlation (future):** Type-stratified theme prevalence provides
   a natural entry point for correlating strategic emphasis with CIHI quality
   indicators within type groups — a more appropriate comparison than cross-type
   analysis.
